The Inflection — What Changed in October 2025
1
Phase 1 (Oct 4, 2025) — Continuous presentation begins
CTS moved from fixed batch sessions to continuous presentation 10AM–4PM. Banks could no longer accumulate cheques and process at night. Inward cheques began flowing continuously throughout the day. Batch-era workflows broke overnight.
→ Every bank's clearing operations team is now on a live, continuous inward feed
2
Phase 2 (Jan 3, 2026) — T+3 hour Item Expiry Time with deemed approval
A cheque presented at 10:00 AM must be confirmed or returned by 1:00 PM. Miss the deadline → deemed approved → bank pays regardless of fraud risk. Hourly settlement cycles. Manual human review at scale became structurally impossible — a reviewer has 2 minutes per cheque at peak load.
→ AI decisioning is no longer optional. It is the only viable path.
3
The trajectory does not stop at T+3 hours
RBI compressed CTS: T+10 days (MICR era) → T+1 day → same-day → T+3 hours — across three mandates. Each step is a pure SLA reduction with zero process change. The logical next steps are T+30 minutes and real-time. A platform built for batch cannot survive this.
→ Our platform responds to any future SLA with a single configuration change — zero code change
The Opportunity Gap — Why Now, Why Us
A
No AI-Native CTS Vendor
Every existing player — Nelito, TCS BaNCS, Finacle — added AI to a compliance product. Nobody has built a CTS platform where agentic AI fraud scoring, signature verification, and swarm processing are the core — not the add-on.
B
Co-op & RRB White Space
~1,500 urban co-operative banks and RRBs are underserved — basic Nelito installs, no AI, no continuous clearing readiness. Decision cycles are faster, procurement lighter. The natural first market.
C
CBS Cannot Be the Bottleneck
500 concurrent inward agents all querying CBS simultaneously would break legacy core banking systems. Our architecture eliminates this — signature vault + PPS vault mean CBS sees only one balance check per cheque. 80% CBS load reduction.
The core thesis: The T+3 hour deemed-approval regime created urgent, unfulfilled demand for real-time AI processing at every bank in India. The window for an AI-native entrant is open now — and closes in 18–24 months as incumbents catch up. Domain depth + NPCI insider knowledge + purpose-built agentic architecture = the moat.

NPCI Process & AI Opportunity Map  ·  Confidential
9 Steps. 9 AI Insertion Points.

The NPCI CTS process is fixed — mandated by RBI circular and NPCI procedural guidelines. AI does not change the process. It occupies every manual step within it, at machine speed.

The Correct CTS Sequence — Presenting Bank → NGCH → Drawee Bank → Returns
1
Presenting Bank
Preliminary verification, sorting, crossing & endorsement
Physical cheque verified, crossed, endorsement printed on reverse (unique instrument ID). Only CTS-2010 compliant instruments proceed. Currently: manual visual inspection under UV lamp.
AI: CTS-2010 compliance check on scanned image  ·  alteration forensics  ·  digital UV-equivalent analysis
2
Presenting Bank
Image capture — 3 images per cheque per RBI specification  ·  amount keyed in manually
Front Grayscale 100 DPI JPEG  ·  Front B&W 200 DPI TIFF  ·  Reverse B&W 200 DPI TIFF. Max 250 instruments per capture file. Amount must be manually keyed to complete the data record.
AI: Auto-OCR all fields including amount in words AND figures  ·  cross-validates both  ·  eliminates manual keying error
3
Presenting Bank → NGCH
Digital signing, encryption, transmission to NGCH  ·  Currently: batch SFTP/API at session windows
PKI/HSM digital signature applied. Submitted to NPCI National Grid Clearing House, Chennai. Cheques accumulate in a batch buffer until the submission window fires — introducing pre-IET latency.
MCP Ask 1 (from NPCI): continuous per-cheque push to NGCH  ·  no batch accumulation  ·  IET starts at max lead time for drawee
4
NGCH / NPCI
Validation, sorting, routing to drawee bank, IET assignment  ·  PPS cross-check at NGCH level
NGCH validates images and MICR data, routes to correct drawee bank, assigns Item Expiry Time. Currently: drawee bank learns of inward cheques only when it next polls NGCH — introducing polling latency.
MCP Ask 2 (from NPCI): NGCH pushes each inward cheque event to drawee agent via WebSocket  ·  no polling  ·  <100ms notification
5
Drawee Bank — Our Platform
Inward receipt → agent swarm → confirm or return within IET  ·  <600ms for any batch size
Images land on bank premises (already local after batch fetch from NGCH). N agents spawn simultaneously via Kafka fan-out. Signature vault + PPS vault lookups (<5ms each). CBS balance check (async). AI fraud score. Decision filed to NGCH.
Platform core: agent swarm + vaults + async CBS  ·  <600ms wall-clock for entire batch
6
Drawee Bank → NGCH
Return filing for dishonoured cheques  ·  Currently: batch accumulation until return session end
Return reason code filed to NGCH. NGCH routes to presenting bank through its existing defined process. Currently delayed until batch return session — hours after AI decision was made.
MCP Ask 3 (from NPCI): instant per-cheque return filing to NGCH  ·  no session accumulation  ·  <2 sec of AI decision
NPCI fee context — why per-cheque pricing is natural
₹1.50 per cheque — NPCI infrastructure fee
₹0.50 charged to presenting bank  +  ₹1.00 charged to drawee bank. No other charges permitted beyond RBI-prescribed rates. Banks already budget in per-cheque cost terms. Our ₹0.12–0.18 software layer is a natural extension — not a new budget category to justify.
NAS — 10-year archival via NPCI API
NPCI's National Archival Services (NAS) stores cheque images for 10 years. Banks access via API. This creates a data substrate for AI-powered dispute resolution and fraud analytics — available today, largely unused by existing platforms.
The process is immutable. The speed is not. Every step above is mandated by NPCI guidelines and RBI circulars. Our platform does not change who acts at which step. It changes only one thing at each step: what took minutes takes milliseconds.

Agentic Platform Architecture  ·  Confidential
One Cheque. One Agent. Under 600ms.

The platform spawns one autonomous agent per inward cheque the moment images land on bank premises. Images are already local — no NGCH network call in the processing clock. 500 cheques → 500 agents simultaneously → entire batch done in under 600 milliseconds.

Why Agentic — Six Hallmarks, All Present in CTS
The six hallmarks — all present
Goal-directed — terminal goal: confirm, return, or escalate before IET. Every tool call serves this goal. Tool-using — calls OCR engine, alteration detector, signature verifier, PPS vault, CBS API, fraud model, NGCH filer Multi-step reasoning — synthesises signals: borderline fraud score + PPS confirmed + 12-yr account = confirm with flag Time-aware — monitors its own IET countdown; if CBS is slow, runs on image signals alone and files before expiry Conditional branching — govt cheque → special session queue; high fraud score → skip CBS → auto-return immediately ⑥Human handoff — packages fraud score rationale, image, CBS data, PPS status → escalates with full context
The swarm — batch time collapses to single-cheque time
Sequential (legacy): 500 cheques × 13 sec each = 108 minutes. Unusable at T+3 hours.
Agent swarm: 500 agents spawn simultaneously via Kafka fan-out. Each processes its own cheque independently. Wall-clock = slowest single agent = <600ms for any batch size.
Infrastructure: Kafka topic fan-out → Kubernetes KEDA auto-scaling → one worker per cheque. Scales linearly to any batch size. Workers terminate on completion — no idle resource waste.

Routing Logic — Three Outcomes, Every Cheque
STP Confirm — Auto
~85% of volume
All extractions high-confidence · no cross-validation flags · fraud score below threshold · signature match above 0.75 · balance sufficient. Agent files confirm to NGCH immediately. No human involvement.
Human Review Queue
~10–12% of volume
Payee OCR below confidence threshold · borderline signature match (0.6–0.75) · minor flag without clear rejection criterion · handwriting ambiguity. Agent packages all signals with rationale. Human decides within IET.
STP Return — Auto
~3–5% of volume
Clear rejection criterion met: stale date · account not found · alteration score above threshold · signature match below 0.4 · stop payment flag active. Agent files return with reason code to NGCH immediately.
The handwriting caveat — stated honestly: Payee name extraction from handwritten cheques is the hardest problem in cheque OCR. Confidence ranges from 60–80% depending on handwriting clarity, regional scripts, and business name formats. This does not cause auto-rejection — it triggers human review. The platform's STP rate claim (85%+) is calibrated around this reality. As the model is trained on more bank-specific data, payee name confidence improves. The progressive learning loop — every human correction feeds back into model retraining — means the platform gets measurably better with every month of production use.

The OEM Fragmentation Problem — Why It Is Structurally Unfixable Without AI
1
Five OEMs. Fifty format variants. No standard.
NCR Atleos, Diebold Nixdorf, Hyosung, AGS Transact, Euronet — each generates EJ logs in proprietary plaintext formats. Worse: each OEM has 3–5 active firmware versions in the field simultaneously, each with slightly different log syntax, field ordering, and error code schemes. A bank with a mixed fleet of 500 ATMs may be dealing with 12–15 distinct EJ dialects. Manual mapping is not just difficult — it is a never-ending treadmill. Every firmware update potentially breaks an existing parser.
→ No unified view · OEM lock-in · Maintenance cost compounds every quarter
2
The dispute timeline is a customer justice failure
A customer's card is debited but cash is not dispensed. They raise a complaint. The bank's ops team raises an internal ticket. A back-office officer manually requests the EJ log from the ATM switch team. The switch team pulls the raw log, tries to interpret it, and packages a PDF. This takes 3–7 days minimum. NPCI's arbitration window is tight — if the bank submits late or with incomplete evidence, they automatically lose the dispute. The customer waits 30–45 days for a resolution that should take 48 hours. In many cases the customer gives up. The bank absorbs the loss silently.
→ Customer trust eroded · Bank absorbs wrongful losses · NPCI arbitration fees paid regardless of outcome
3
Fraud signals are invisible in raw EJ data
Skimming devices trigger characteristic card-read sequences in the EJ. Cash trapping produces specific dispense-attempt patterns. Suspicious engineer access — outside normal maintenance windows, from unknown technician IDs — is logged but never monitored. Cassette swap irregularities appear as denomination mismatches across sessions. All of this is in the EJ. None of it is read systematically because the data is unstructured, fragmented, and buried across thousands of files.
→ Fraud detected weeks late · Prevention impossible · Post-incident losses avoidable in hindsight
4
Regulatory compliance is a fire drill, not a capability
RBI can ask a bank for 6 months of ATM transaction logs at any time — during audits, incident investigations, or regulatory reviews. Most banks cannot produce a clean, complete, structured response within 24 hours. What they produce instead is a collection of raw log files with a disclaimer. This is not compliance — it is hope. The gap between what regulators expect and what banks can actually deliver is significant and growing.
→ Regulatory risk · Audit cost · Senior management time on fire-fighting

Standardisation & AI — The Classic Use Case  ·  Prepared by: Nilesh Shah
Why AI is the Textbook Answer Here

EJ standardisation is one of the clearest cases where AI is not just useful — it is structurally the only viable solution. The problem is unstructured data with high variance and continuous drift. That is precisely the problem domain where AI outperforms every rule-based alternative.

Why Rule-Based Parsers Always Fail
The rule engine problem — a treadmill with no exit
A rule-based EJ parser works by mapping known field positions and delimiters to a target schema. This works — until the OEM releases a firmware update that shifts a field by two characters, adds a new error code, or changes the timestamp format. The parser silently produces wrong output. Nobody notices until a dispute is lost or a compliance audit fails. The bank's IT team patches the rule, and the cycle repeats. For a bank with 5 OEM types × 4 firmware versions × 500 ATMs, maintaining a rule-based system is a full-time job that never ends and never catches up.
The silent failure mode: Rule engines fail without error — they produce plausible-looking output that is subtly wrong. This is worse than a crash. Wrong data in the canonical store is harder to detect and fix than missing data.
Why AI is structurally superior for this problem
Variance tolerance — AI model generalises across format variants without explicit rules for each Firmware drift resilience — model retrained on new samples, not reprogrammed field by field Confidence scoring — AI outputs a confidence score per field; low-confidence extractions queue for human review rather than silently failing Active learning loop — every human correction feeds back into model retraining; the system gets measurably better over time Anomaly detection as a byproduct — the same model that learns normal EJ patterns automatically flags deviations: skimming signatures, cash trap sequences, unusual access patterns
The Canonical EJ Schema — What a Normalised Record Looks Like
Canonical EJ Record — every ATM, every OEM, one schema
atm_id: "ATM-MH-0042"
oem: "NCR" · fw_version: "7.4.2"
txn_id: "TXN20260610142307"
timestamp: "2026-06-10T14:23:07.421Z"
txn_type: "CASH_WITHDRAWAL"
card_hash: "sha256:a3f9...c4e2" (masked)
amount_requested: 5000
amount_dispensed: 5000
dispense_outcome: "SUCCESS"
cassette_id: "C2" · denomination: 500
error_code: null
retract_occurred: false
card_read_attempts: 1
engineer_access_flag: false
record_hash: "sha256:prev_hash+fields"
parsing_confidence: 0.97
The AI Parsing Pipeline — Raw Log to Canonical Record
Step 1Ingest
EJ Collector AgentAsync file watcher on bank's existing network share. Picks up new EJ files as they arrive — no ATM-side change, no switch-side change. Supports SFTP, NFS, SMB, direct path.
OEM ClassifierFirst model: "which OEM and firmware version is this file?" — trained on labeled samples from all 5 OEMs. Confidence >0.95 routes to extractor. Below threshold → human review queue.
Pre-processorEncoding normalisation (UTF-8, ASCII, CP1252), line-ending normalisation, file integrity check, duplicate detection by file hash.
Step 2Extract
Field Extractor ModelSequence labelling model (BiLSTM or fine-tuned transformer) extracts all canonical fields from the raw log. Outputs structured JSON per transaction with per-field confidence scores.
ValidatorCross-checks extracted fields for internal consistency: amount_requested ≥ amount_dispensed, timestamp within ATM operating hours, card_hash format valid, error_code in known registry.
Active Learning LoopLow-confidence records (any field <0.85) routed to human review UI. Human correction stored as labeled training sample. Model retrained weekly on accumulated corrections.
Step 3Store & Index
Canonical StoreHash-chained PostgreSQL records — each record includes SHA-256 hash of (previous_hash + all_fields). Tamper-evident by construction. Immutable once written.
Hot CacheRedis index on ATM ID, transaction ID, card hash, and date range — enables sub-second lookup for dispute resolution and NPCI queries.
Anomaly EngineLocal LLM running on-prem (ONNX runtime, no cloud) scores each transaction for skimming signatures, cash trap patterns, unusual sequences. Flags reviewed by security team dashboard.
The data moat compounds: Every labeled correction from human review enriches the training set. Every new OEM firmware version encountered adds to the model's generalisation capability. A platform with 2 years of production data across 20 banks is not just better than a new entrant — it is structurally unreplicable without the same data investment.

NPCI Arbitration — From Manual to Self-Clearing  ·  Prepared by: Nilesh Shah
What If Disputes Resolved Themselves?

The arbitration process exists because evidence is unstructured, manually assembled, and asymmetrically available. Remove those three conditions — and the need for human adjudication collapses for the majority of cases. That is what normalised EJ data, CCTV auto-extraction, and a shared evidence protocol make possible.

Categories that auto-resolve
✓Cash dispensed + card debited — EJ shows SUCCESS dispense, CBS shows debit. Auto-close. No dispute valid. Customer notified instantly. ✓Cash NOT dispensed + card debited — EJ shows FAILED/RETRACT, CBS shows debit. Auto-credit customer. Bank files for interchange recovery. ✓Partial dispense + full debit — EJ shows partial cassette failure mid-dispense. Auto-partial credit for undispensed amount. Customer notified. ~Disputed amount mismatch — EJ shows different amount than CBS. Routes to human review with both records surfaced. CCTV evidence auto-pulled.
The aspiration — NPCI as exception handler only
If all banks adopt canonical EJ and expose a standardised evidence API to NPCI, the arbitration calculus changes fundamentally. NPCI's dispute portal queries the canonical store directly — no bank ops team involvement, no PDF submissions, no waiting for manual evidence assembly. Clear-cut cases — which are the large majority — auto-resolve within 48 hours. NPCI adjudicators handle only genuine edge cases: disputed sequences, contested CCTV evidence, multi-party fraud. Arbitration fees drop. Customer resolution time drops from 45 days to 48 hours. Network trust improves for everyone. This is not a product feature — it is a structural upgrade to how India's ATM dispute ecosystem works.
CCTV Integration — Automated Evidence Extraction and Packaging
The CCTV problem today
Every RBI-mandated ATM has CCTV — typically 1–2 cameras (front-facing customer, cash slot). Footage is stored on local DVR/NVR at the ATM site or branch, retained for 30–90 days per bank policy. When a dispute is raised, the bank's security team must manually identify the ATM, access the DVR system, locate the correct time window, clip the footage, and submit it. Timestamps on DVRs are frequently miscalibrated. Footage is often overwritten before the request is fulfilled. The evidence that should be definitive — the customer standing at the machine — arrives late, incomplete, or not at all.
How our platform automates CCTV evidence
The canonical EJ record contains a precise transaction timestamp — to the millisecond. When a dispute is flagged, the platform automatically:

Step 1: Identifies the transaction window from the EJ record (e.g. 14:23:07 to 14:23:52)
Step 2: Sends an API call to the ATM's DVR/NVR management system — "extract camera 1 footage, ATM-MH-0042, from 14:21:00 to 14:26:00, 2026-06-10" — a 5-minute window with buffer
Step 3: Clips are hashed and attached to the dispute evidence package alongside the canonical EJ record and CBS debit confirmation
Step 4: When NPCI requests evidence for a specific dispute, the complete package — EJ record + CCTV clip + CBS record — is submitted programmatically within minutes
Technical requirements + honest caveats
What's needed: DVR/NVR must support an API — Hikvision, Dahua, CP Plus (dominant in India) all support REST or ONVIF protocol. Most ATM CCTV installed post-2018 qualifies.

What doesn't need changing: ATM hardware, ATM software, camera hardware, recording settings — nothing changes for the bank's security team.

Honest caveat: Older analogue DVR systems (pre-2016 installs) do not have APIs. CCTV integration is an enhancement layer — the platform works fully without it. Banks can enable it progressively as they upgrade their surveillance infrastructure.

Privacy: Footage extracted only for specific dispute cases on NPCI request. Never stored in canonical store permanently. Transmitted over encrypted channel. Auto-deleted after dispute resolution period.
The vision: A customer disputes a transaction at 10:00 AM. By 10:05 AM, the platform has assembled the canonical EJ record, the CBS debit confirmation, and a CCTV clip of the transaction. NPCI's system queries the evidence API. The dispute is auto-resolved by 10:15 AM. The customer receives an SMS credit notification before they have left the bank branch. This is not science fiction — it is the logical end-state of normalised ATM data infrastructure.
